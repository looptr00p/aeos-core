# STATE_CLASSIFICATION_POLICY.md

## Purpose

Prevent state ambiguity, capability inflation, and epistemic drift by explicitly distinguishing operational maturity levels when describing AEOS systems, architecture, workflows, capabilities, governance models, or operational cognition patterns.

## CORE RULE

When explaining AEOS systems, architecture, workflows, capabilities, governance models, repositories, or operational cognition patterns, ALWAYS classify statements into one of these four states:

- IMPLEMENTED
- EXPERIMENTAL
- PROPOSED
- STRATEGIC

Never collapse these states together.

---

## State Definitions

### IMPLEMENTED

**Definition**: Operationally real, repository-backed, active, and verifiable.

**Epistemic Level**: VERIFIABLE

**Characteristics**:
- exists today
- operationally active
- auditable
- repository-grounded
- validated through use
- maintainable by current workflows

**Examples**:
- active governance workflows
- implemented lifecycle systems
- operational memory structures
- enforced review processes
- existing lint validation
- validated prompts
- repository-backed operational policies

**Required Behavior**:
- speak concretely
- reference operational reality
- avoid speculative wording
- prefer repository evidence over assumptions

**Forbidden Behavior**:
- exaggerating maturity
- implying scalability not yet proven
- inferring missing implementation details

---

### EXPERIMENTAL

**Definition**: Actively explored concepts, patterns, or governance models under evaluation.

**Epistemic Level**: EXPLORATORY

**Characteristics**:
- partially validated
- unstable
- evolving
- may change significantly
- may never become canonical

**Examples**:
- governance experiments
- cognition heuristics under testing
- observability research
- workflow prototypes
- entropy reduction experiments
- alternative operational models

**Required Behavior**:
- explicitly acknowledge uncertainty
- describe experiments as non-canonical
- separate findings from established policy

**Forbidden Behavior**:
- presenting experiments as operational truth
- implying guaranteed future adoption
- overstating validation quality

---

### PROPOSED

**Definition**: Designed or planned systems not yet implemented operationally.

**Epistemic Level**: DESIGNED

**Characteristics**:
- architecturally intentional
- governance review pending
- implementation incomplete or absent
- operational viability not yet proven

**Examples**:
- future governance systems
- planned lifecycle extensions
- proposed observability architectures
- conceptual operational tooling
- roadmap governance models

**Required Behavior**:
- clearly identify as planned or conceptual
- distinguish proposals from current capabilities
- acknowledge implementation status

**Forbidden Behavior**:
- describing proposals as existing systems
- implying operational readiness
- masking implementation gaps

---

### STRATEGIC

**Definition**: Long-horizon AEOS direction, philosophy, or conceptual trajectory.

**Epistemic Level**: DIRECTIONAL

**Characteristics**:
- directional
- philosophical
- longitudinal
- not constrained by current repository scope
- may span multiple future operational stages

**Examples**:
- governance-first AI systems
- operational cognition architecture
- longitudinal governance research
- anti-entropy doctrine
- future canonical operational models
- human-governed AI ecosystems

**Required Behavior**:
- frame as vision or strategic direction
- avoid implying immediate implementation
- preserve distinction from operational reality

**Forbidden Behavior**:
- collapsing strategy into implementation
- implying roadmap certainty
- overstating current maturity

---

## Repository Truth Supremacy

Repository state is the primary operational truth source.

If repository state conflicts with embedded assumptions:
- trust repository evidence
- acknowledge discrepancy
- assume embedded knowledge may be stale

Never infer:
- implementation maturity from strategic intent
- operational capability from research activity
- governance legitimacy from conceptual sophistication

---

## Governance Objective

This policy exists to reduce:
- state ambiguity
- capability inflation
- governance drift
- hallucinated maturity
- roadmap confusion
- operational misrepresentation
- reviewer uncertainty

---

## Epistemic Governance Principle

The system must explicitly communicate:
- what type of truth is being stated
- what level of validation exists
- what level of operational legitimacy exists
- what degree of uncertainty remains

Operational clarity is more important than appearing advanced.

---

## Required Classification Behavior

When explaining AEOS:

1. Separate IMPLEMENTED, EXPERIMENTAL, PROPOSED, and STRATEGIC claims.
2. Explicitly label uncertain areas.
3. Prefer repository-grounded evidence.
4. Avoid collapsing roadmap, research, and implementation together.
5. Preserve governance legitimacy over capability expansion.

---
